Galileo Galilei

1564 – 1642  ·  Father of Modern Science

Introduction

Who Was Galileo Galilei?

Galileo Galilei was an Italian astronomer, physicist, and mathematician born in Pisa, Italy in 1564. He is widely regarded as the father of modern science — the first person to systematically combine careful observation, controlled experimentation, and mathematical reasoning to understand the natural world. In an era when scholars relied on ancient texts as the final authority on how the universe worked, Galileo looked through a telescope and trusted what he saw. What he found changed everything: mountains on the Moon, moons orbiting Jupiter, the phases of Venus, and proof that the Earth was not the center of the universe. He paid a steep price for these discoveries — tried by the Inquisition, forced to recant, and confined to house arrest for the final years of his life. But he was right, and history has never forgotten it.

Significance

Why Does He Matter to Astronomy?

Before Galileo, astronomy was largely a theoretical discipline — scholars calculated where celestial objects ought to be, but few thought to observe them directly with instruments. Galileo introduced the telescope to serious astronomical research and used it to gather evidence that dismantled the 2,000-year-old geocentric model of the universe. His support for the Copernican heliocentric model — that the Earth orbits the Sun, not the other way around — laid the foundation for everything that came after: Newton's laws of gravity, Kepler's orbital mechanics, and eventually the space age. Every telescope ever launched, every spacecraft trajectory ever calculated, and every physics equation taught in a classroom today carries a debt to the work Galileo began in the early 1600s. Stephen Hawking called him "perhaps more than any other single person, responsible for the birth of modern science."
